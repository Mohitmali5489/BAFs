Financial Accounting II - Very Imp Theory

1. What is Consignment and Parties to consignment

Consignment is when one person (the consignor) gives goods to another person or business (the consignee) to sell on their behalf. The consignor still owns the goods until they are sold. Once sold, the consignee takes a commission and gives the rest of the money to the consignor.

Parties in Consignment:
1. Consignor – The owner of the goods who sends them for sale (e.g., a manufacturer or wholesaler).
2. Consignee – The person or business that receives the goods and sells them (e.g., a shop or dealer).

Example: A clothing brand (consignor) sends clothes to a boutique (consignee). The boutique sells the clothes and gives the money (minus commission) back to the brand.

2. Types of Commission in Consignment (Distinguish May Come)

1. Ordinary Commission: A fixed percentage of the total sales value.
Example: If the commission is 5% and goods worth ₹10,000 are sold, the commission is ₹500.

2. Del Credere Commission: Extra commission given to the consignee for taking responsibility for bad debts.
Example: If a consignee gets 5% ordinary commission and 2% del credere commission, they earn extra for ensuring all payments are collected.

3. Overriding Commission: Additional commission given for selling goods at a higher price, selling special products, or reaching a sales target.
Example: A consignee may get an extra 3% commission for selling a premium brand or exceeding a monthly sales goal.

3. Difference Between Branch Accounts and Departmental Accounts

Basis Branch Accounts Departmental Accounts
Meaning Accounts maintained for different branches of a business located at different places. Accounts maintained for different departments within the same business location.
Location Different geographical locations (e.g., Mumbai branch, Delhi branch). Different divisions within the same premises (e.g., Electronics Dept., Clothing Dept.).
Autonomy Branches may have some independence in operations. Departments work under the same management.
Purpose To know the profit/loss of each branch separately. To determine the profitability of each department.
Accounting Treatment Can be maintained as dependent or independent branch accounts. A single combined set of books is maintained with department-wise records.
Inter-Transactions Goods or cash may be transferred between branches. Goods and expenses may be shared between departments.
Examples A retail company with branches in multiple cities. A departmental store selling clothing, footwear, and electronics.

3. Difference Between Statement of Affairs and Balance Sheet

Point of Difference Statement of Affairs Balance Sheet
Purpose To find the capital at the start or end of the year. To know the financial position on a specific date.
Capital Calculated as a difference between assets and liabilities. Taken from proper accounts in the ledger.
Basis Based on incomplete records, like single entry or estimates. Based on complete records using double entry system.
Accuracy Not very accurate, as many figures are estimated. Very accurate and verified from records.
Reliability Less reliable due to missing or estimated data. More reliable and trusted by outsiders.
Use Used in small businesses or when records are not fully maintained. Used by all types of businesses, especially large ones.
Format No fixed format. Has a standard format.
Verification Hard to verify figures. Easy to verify through books and documents.

5. Types of Branches in Branch Accounts

1. Dependent Branch: The branch does not keep its own accounts; all transactions are recorded by the head office (HO). The branch only maintains stock and cash records. The HO sends goods and manages all major expenses.
Example: A clothing store with multiple outlets, where all decisions are controlled by the main office.

2. Independent Branch: The branch maintains its own accounts and prepares financial statements. It can purchase goods, manage expenses, and handle cash independently. At the end of the year, it sends reports to the HO.
Example: A multinational company with separate branches in different countries, each managing its own finances.

3. Foreign Branch: A branch located in a different country, following local currency and accounting rules. It operates like an independent branch but must report to the head office in the parent company’s currency.
Example: An Indian company with a branch in the USA.

4. Wholesale Branch: Sells goods in bulk (wholesale) instead of directly to customers. Helps expand distribution without relying on third-party wholesalers.
Example: A manufacturer’s branch that supplies goods to retailers instead of individual customers.
